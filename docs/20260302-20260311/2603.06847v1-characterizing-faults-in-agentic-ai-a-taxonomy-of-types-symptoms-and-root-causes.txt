# Characterizing Faults in Agentic AI: A Taxonomy of Types, Symptoms, and Root Causes 

MEHIL B SHAH, Dalhousie University, Canada 

MOHAMMAD MEHDI MOROVATI, Polytechnique Montreal, Canada 

MOHAMMAD MASUDUR RAHMAN, Dalhousie University, Canada 

FOUTSE KHOMH, Polytechnique Montreal, Canada 

Context: Agentic AI systems combine large language model (LLM) reasoning with tool invocation and long-horizon control. This architectural composition introduces failure modes that differ from those in traditional software systems and standalone LLM-based applications. Despite their growing use in mission-critical settings, there is limited empirical understanding of how faults in agentic AI systems arise, propagate across components, and align with developer experience. 

Objective: This paper aims to empirically characterise faults in agentic AI systems and to derive actionable taxonomies of fault types, observable symptoms, and root causes. We further analyse fault propagation across system components and evaluate the extent to which the resulting taxonomy reflects practitioners’ real-world experiences. 

Method: We analysed 13,602 closed issues and merged pull requests from 40 open-source agentic AI repositories. Using stratified sampling, we selected 385 faults for in-depth qualitative analysis. We applied grounded theory to derive taxonomies of fault types, symptoms, and root causes, and used Apriori-based association rule mining to identify statistically significant fault propagation patterns. The taxonomy was subsequently validated through a developer study with 145 practitioners. 

Results: Our analysis produced a comprehensive taxonomy comprising five architectural fault dimensions, 13 symptom classes, and 12 root cause categories. Association rule mining revealed recurring, high-lift propagation pathways, demonstrating that faults in agentic AI systems frequently traverse architectural boundaries—for example, from token management logic to authentication failures, from datetime handling to scheduling anomalies, and from state management defects to memory-related symptoms. The developer study provided strong empirical validation: practitioners rated the taxonomy as highly representative of real-world failures (mean = 3.97/5, Cronbach’s 𝛼 = 0.904), and 83.8% reported that it covered faults they had personally encountered. Qualitative feedback suggested targeted refinements, particularly around multi-agent coordination and observability, while largely affirming the taxonomy’s completeness. 

Conclusion: This study shows that failures in agentic AI systems are structured rather than ad hoc, exhibiting a distinctive hybrid failure profile that combines conventional software engineering faults with probabilistic LLM-driven behaviour. These failures often propagate across system layers, are exacerbated by limited observability and ecosystem fragility, and yet follow consistent patterns that can be systematically exploited. By grounding failure analysis in real-world evidence and practitioner validation, this work establishes a foundation for principled debugging, observability, and reliability engineering tailored to agentic AI systems. 1 Introduction 

Recent years have seen growing interest in building more capable AI systems that can do more than simply engaging in passive conversation. This shift has led to the emergence of agentic AI systems that can plan actions and use external tools to achieve complex goals [ 41 , 43 ]. Industry increasingly deploys these agentic AI systems in important domains such as autonomous software engineering, enterprise automation, robotics, and real-world decision support [ 23 , 24 , 34 ]. Unlike conversational LLM systems, agentic systems execute multi-step reasoning, maintain internal state across interactions, and actively interact with external environments to accomplish their tasks. This operational independence allows us to automate routine tasks more effectively, improve efficiency in complex workflows, and achieve more scalable system behaviour; at the same time, it introduces substantial challenges for reliability, correctness, and safety. Because agentic systems differ fundamentally from both traditional deterministic software and LLM-based conversational systems, they may manifest their failures differently. In traditional software and conversational LLM systems, failures typically arise from faulty code or model hallucinations, respectively [ 14 , 22 , 45 ]. In contrast, in agentic systems, failures arise not only from faulty code or model hallucinations, but also from agent orchestration, evolving internal state, and interactions with environmental feedback [ 8]. An agent may, for example, generate a seemingly reasonable sequence of actions, yet misinterpret tool outputs, become trapped in infinite reasoning cycles, or maintain incorrect or outdated assumptions about the external file-system state [ 17 ]. Recent industry reports and security advisories document incidents in which the agentic systems deleted critical data, failed to terminate processes, or hallucinated API parameters, resulting in security vulnerabilities and economic losses [ 33 ]. These failures exacerbate the existing challenges and costs of poor quality software and bugs (e.g., the US economy loses $2.4T per year) [ 10 , 39 ]. Beyond these economic consequences, agentic systems introduce risks that extend to physical safety and system integrity. When deployed in safety-critical domains such as healthcare, transportation, industry, and energy infrastructure, failures involving misinterpreted tool outputs, uncontrolled action execution, or agents operating on incorrect system state can directly lead to physical harm, cascading system failures, or loss of lives [ 6 ]. Without a clear understanding of how these failures arise, manifest, and propagate through agentic workflows, deploying such systems in real-world environments is inherently risky and, in extreme cases, potentially fatal [ 4 , 6]. These concerns have prompted growing attention from both industry and academia toward the systematic study of agentic system failures. Several prior studies have investigated failures in agentic systems. Cemri et al. [ 8 ] analyse failures in multi-agent LLM systems and emphasize coordination challenges, but their analysis overlooks the mapping between agentic failures and system components. Rahardja et al. [ 34 ] study software engineering agents, evaluate their capability to generate patches against issue reports, and propose a taxonomy derived from GitHub issues. However, their taxonomy is focused towards guiding automated repair workflows and benchmarking patch-generation success, and thus fails to explain how faults arise within specific agent components or how they propagate through different components. Lu et al. [ 24 ] study autonomous agents in the software engineering domain, analysing failures in programming tasks such as web crawling and data analysis. Using the failure

logs, they propose a taxonomy of agent faults. While these advancements are inspiring, the lack of a connection between failures and components in agentic systems limits the effectiveness of the existing taxonomies. In other words, establishing such links is essential to enable systematic diagnosis of agentic failures and the development of appropriate debugging techniques. In this paper, we conduct a large-scale empirical study of faults in real-world agentic AI systems and examine where , how , and why they arise. Prior studies have largely analysed faults at the task or behavioural level; in contrast, our work adopts a component-grounded perspective that explicitly links agentic faults to system components and identifies the underlying causes of these faults and their observable symptoms. We design our study in five steps as follows. First , we construct a dataset of 13,602 documented faults from 40 real-world agentic systems. Second , we apply stratified sampling to select 385 faults for in-depth manual analysis, preserving representativeness across repository types (e.g., frameworks, applications). Third , we use a grounded theory approach [ 13 ] to inductively derive taxonomies of fault types, symptom patterns, and root causes through iterative coding and comparison. Fourth , we apply Apriori-based association rule mining [ 3 ] to identify statistically significant relationships among bug types, symptoms, and root causes, revealing fault propagation patterns. Finally , we validate the resulting taxonomies through a structured developer study with 145 participants experienced in agentic system development, establishing ecological validity and practical utility. We also answer the following research questions (RQs) in our study: 

RQ1: What are the characteristics, observable symptoms, and root causes of bugs and failures in real-world agentic AI systems? 

Prior analyses of failures in agentic AI systems have largely focused on task outcomes or high-level behavioural errors. Advancing the understanding of agentic AI failures, therefore, requires a systematic identification of fault types, observable symptoms, and root causes. By analysing 385 representative, we identify 5 fault dimensions, 13 symptom classes, and 12 root cause categories. Faults in Cognitive Control and Agency components indicate failures in agent decision-making and control flows, while the high incidence of faults in Runtime and Environment Grounding components (87 instances) indicates the fragility of the surrounding execution ecosystem. Dominant root causes— 

Dependency and Integration Failures (19.5%) and Data and Type Handling Failures (17.6%)—reveal systematic contract violations between probabilistically generated artifacts and the deterministic interface, type, and runtime constraints enforced by external software systems. 

RQ2: What statistically significant associations exist between fault types, observable symptoms, and root causes in agentic AI systems, and what do these associations reveal about how faults manifest and propagate across system components? 

Existing studies typically define failure categories [ 8, 24 , 34 ] without examining how faults originate in agent components and manifest as symptoms in the system. As a result, the mechanisms by which different faults propagate through agentic AI systems remain poorly understood. Using association rule mining, we identify statistically significant associations among fault type, symptom, and root causes. For example, authentication-related request failures are strongly associated with fragile token refresh mechanisms that mishandle credential expiration (lift = 181.5), while incorrect time values typically stem from inappropriate datetime conversions (lift = 121.0). Taken together, these patterns suggest that many faults do not fail transparently; instead, weak error handling and limited logging obscure their underlying causes, turning simple implementation mistakes into difficult-to-diagnose faults. 

RQ3: To what extent do the derived taxonomies of bugs, symptoms, and root causes align with the practical experiences of agentic system developers? 

To assess ecological validity beyond offline analysis, we investigate whether the derived tax-onomies reflect how practitioners characterize and reason about failures in real agentic systems. We conducted a structured developer survey in which practitioners rated the practical relevance of each

fault category based on their experience and provided open-ended feedback on missing or mischar-acterised failures. The developer study suggests strong practical alignment, with a mean relevance rating of 3.97 out of 5, indicating that most fault categories were viewed as relevant in practice, a large majority of ratings (74.5%) at 4 or higher reflecting broad endorsement, and high internal con-sistency (Cronbach’s 𝛼 = 0.91 ) indicating coherent and stable evaluations across categories. Most respondents (83.8%) reported that the taxonomy covered the faults they had encountered, while qualitative feedback suggested refinements (e.g., semantic failures and multi-agent coordination issues). Together, these findings complement the taxonomy by validating its core coverage while identifying targeted refinements that improve its completeness and practical applicability. Thus, we make the following contributions in the paper: (a) An empirically grounded, hierarchical taxonomy of faults, symptoms, and root causes, organ-ised into five high-level dimensions aligned with agentic components. (b) A fault propagation analysis using Apriori-based association rule mining that reveals statisti-cally significant relationships among bug types, symptoms, and root causes. (c) A developer study with 145 participants, establishing the ecological validity and practical utility of the derived taxonomy. The remainder of the paper is organised as follows. Section 2 describes the methodology, including dataset construction, stratified sampling, grounded theory analysis, association rule mining, and the developer study design. Section 3 presents our findings by answering the three research questions, including the derived taxonomies and statistically significant associations among bugs, symptoms, and root causes. Section 4 discusses the implications for debugging, reliability engineering, and agentic system design. Section 5 outlines threats to validity. 2 Methodology 

Figure 1 presents a schematic overview of our study workflow. We describe each step below. 

2.1 Repository Selection 

To curate a representative, high-quality dataset of agentic AI repositories, we followed the repository selection methodology from prior work [ 34 ]. We queried GitHub for repositories associated with the term “AI agents” that were active as of June 2025. To focus on robust, widely used systems, we retained repositories with more than 1,000 stars and at least 30 issues. These thresholds target projects with sustained maintenance and community engagement, filtering out transient or inactive repositories. This screening yielded 82 candidates. We then applied a language constraint to align with the dominant agent-development ecosystem: because Python is the predominant language for agentic workflows [ 16 ], we excluded repositories implemented in other languages. This reduced the candidate set from 82 to 47. After automated filtering, we conducted manual annotation to ensure that the dataset comprised only agentic AI frameworks, libraries, or tools. To assess inter-rater agreement, we used Cohen’s Kappa [ 12 ], a standard measure in similar studies [ 28 ]. In the first round, two annotators inde-pendently labelled all repositories, yielding a Cohen’s Kappa of 0.32 (fair agreement) [ 26 ]. The annotators then reconciled disagreements and refined inclusion criteria. A second round produced a Cohen’s Kappa of 0.83 (substantial agreement). This process resulted in a final dataset of 40 Agentic AI repositories.

2.3 Dataset Construction 

To reflect the diversity of the agentic AI ecosystem and support generalisability, we constructed a corpus of 40 repositories through a category-agnostic search process, screening each candidate for relevance to agentic systems without imposing architectural constraints during discovery. Once the final set of repositories was established, we conducted open coding to classify each repository according to its primary architectural role. This classification was guided by the IEEE Standard Glossary of Software Engineering Terminology and established repository taxonomies [ 1 , 19 ]. This process yielded four categories— Frameworks , Applications , Libraries , and Tools —which were used solely to characterise ecosystem coverage rather than to guide repository selection or issue retrieval. The Cohen’s kappa for the process was 0.86, which indicates near-perfect agreement [26]. Because our filtered corpus comprises 13,602 items, conducting a full manual analysis was infea-sible. We therefore employed stratified random sampling to construct a statistically representative subset. The target sample size was set to 385, which yields a 95% confidence level with a 5% margin of error, a commonly accepted threshold in empirical software engineering studies [38]. To preserve the underlying distribution, we applied stratified sampling across the four categories so that each category’s share in the sample matched its share in the population. The final dataset contains 385 faults (issues + PRs): 234 from Frameworks, 75 from Libraries, 39 from Tools, and 37 

from Applications. 

2.4 Grounded Theory and Taxonomy Development 

To develop a comprehensive taxonomy of faults in agentic AI systems, we employed a grounded theory approach [ 13 ] for our analysis. This inductive approach derives categories directly from empirical data rather than relying on an existing taxonomy. We manually examined all 385 sampled items in detail, focussing on reported fault types, observable symptoms, and documented root causes. To reduce subjectivity and maintain consistency, the analysis was conducted iteratively by the first and second authors. We reviewed the items in batches of 25 and met after each batch to clarify ambiguous cases, resolve disagreements, and refine shared coding guidelines. This process allowed the coding scheme to evolve as new agent-specific fault modes emerged, such as errors from autonomous reasoning loops (e.g., microsoft/autogen#4020 ) or failures in LLM integration pathways (e.g., microsoft/autogen#4770 ). We developed the taxonomy through a three-stage coding process as follows: 

• Open Coding: We assign descriptive labels to the raw artefacts, including issue descriptions, execution logs, stack traces, and corresponding fix commits. These labels captured fine-grained technical phenomena, such as API parameter mismatches (e.g., letta-ai/letta#517 ), 

token-limit overflows (e.g., microsoft/autogen#4770 ), and dependency version conflicts (e.g., 

crewAIInc/crewAI#1877 ). 

• Axial Coding: We then examined relationships among these labels to identify conceptual connections and shared underlying causes. Related open codes were grouped into broader sub-categories that reflected operation of agentic AI systems. For example, LLM configuration and API token-handling faults were consolidated under LLM integration problems, while data-type and encoding-related faults were grouped as interpretive errors.

• Selective Coding: Finally, we synthesised these sub-categories into higher-level dimensions aligned with major architectural components of agentic AI systems, such as the cognitive control loop, tool and environment interaction layer and the runtime environment. Through this process, we distilled 385 individual faults into a coherent taxonomy. The resulting structure captures both low-level technical faults and broader architectural issues, reflecting dis-tinctive failure modes ranging from breakdowns in internal reasoning to integration errors and vulnerabilities in the operating environment. 

2.5 Apriori Analysis 

To identify systematic relationships among fault categories, observable symptoms, and root causes, we used the Apriori algorithm [ 3 ]. Apriori is a widely used data mining technique that finds frequent itemsets in transactional data and generates association rules describing how attributes co-occur. In a rule, the antecedent denotes the observed condition(s), and the consequent denotes the attribute(s) that tend to co-occur with those conditions within the same fault instances. In our context, the antecedent can be a fault category or a symptom, and the consequent can be a symptom or a root cause. For example, a rule of the form Symptom = Redundant Tracking → Root Cause = Wrong Default Component Configuration indicates that faults exhibiting redundant tracking frequently have this specific root cause in our dataset. We encoded each of the 385 analysed faults as a transaction with three categorical attributes: 

Fault Category , Symptom , and Root Cause . For example, the following transaction indicates that an LLM Configuration fault can manifest as Redundant Token Tracking and is caused by a Wrong Default Component Config (e.g., deepset-ai/haystack#4944 ): 

Fault Category = LLM Configuration ,

Symptom = Redundant Tracking ,

Root Cause = Wrong Default Component Configuration .

To quantify the strength and reliability of each rule, we computed standard association met-rics [11]: 

• Support measures how frequently items in the antecedent and the consequent co-occur in the dataset. For a rule 𝑋 → 𝑌 , support is defined as the proportion of transactions in which both 𝑋 and 𝑌 appear together. 

• Confidence measures how likely the consequent is to occur when the antecedent is present. Formally, for a rule 𝑋 → 𝑌 , confidence is the conditional probability that a transaction containing 𝑋 also contains 𝑌 .By filtering for high-confidence rules, we extracted a concise set of associations that summarise relationships among faults, symptoms, and root causes. For instance, if we observe a high-confidence rule such as Symptom = Execution Loop → Root Cause = Incorrect Termination Condition . This suggests that, in our dataset, faults that exhibit execution loops are frequently associated with termination misconfigurations. While these rules do not establish causality, they provide empirical evidence to guide root-cause investigation by highlighting statistically significant symptom–cause associations. These rules provide a systematic way to infer likely causes from observed symptoms, improving the interpretability and practical utility of the taxonomy. 

2.6 Developer Study 

To assess the comprehensiveness of our proposed taxonomy, we conducted a qualitative survey with practitioners actively developing agentic AI systems. The survey evaluates whether the taxonomy captures the real-world faults that they routinely encounter.

2.6.1 Instrument Design. To gather structured feedback from the participants, we designed an online questionnaire targeting different aspects of our proposed taxonomy. The survey was divided into three sections. The first section collected demographic information , including participants’ current professional role, total years of experience in software engineering, and experience with agentic AI frameworks such as AutoGen [ 27 ], LangChain [ 20 ], and BabyAGI [ 5]. This information allowed us to assess respondents’ expertise and ensure that feedback came from individuals with relevant experience. The second section focused on taxonomy evaluation . Participants reviewed the full hierarchical taxonomy and rated their agreement on a five-point Likert scale at each level of granularity — i.e, for every top-level category, subcategory, and individual fault type. This provided fine-grained quantitative evidence of alignment between the taxonomy and practitioners’ experience of agentic faults across all levels, rather than only at an aggregate or structural level. The final section collected 

open-ended feedback on completeness, inviting participants to describe missing faults, symptoms, or root causes and suggest additions or refinements. Before administering the main survey, we conducted a pilot study with two academic researchers and two industry practitioners. Feedback from the pilot led to refinements in category definitions to reduce ambiguity and improve interpretability. For example, we revised several category descrip-tions to include clearer boundary conditions and short illustrative examples, ensuring that similar but distinct concepts were more explicitly differentiated. We estimated the survey would take approximately 10–15 minutes. The full questionnaire is available in the replication package [37]. 

2.6.2 Participant Selection. To ensure high-quality and relevant responses, we employed targeted recruitment. First, we identified active contributors to the selected repositories. Using public commit histories, we extracted the public email addresses of top contributors and sent personalised invita-tions. This ensured participants were familiar with practical challenges in agentic AI development. Second, to broaden reach, we disseminated the survey through online communities frequented by AI developers, including specialised Discord servers and Reddit forums dedicated to LLMs and autonomous agents. This combination of direct recruitment and community outreach helped us capture feedback from the individuals with relevant experience. 

2.6.3 Demographics. A total of 145 participants completed the survey, representing diverse profes-sional backgrounds, experience levels, and framework expertise. By affiliation , 76.1% were from industry and 23.9% from academia. Among them, 29.0% reported more than 5 years of experience in software engineering, and 47.8% reported at least 1 year of experience in agentic AI systems. Participants reported familiarity with a range of agentic AI frameworks. The most common were LangChain (69.6%) and AutoGen (34.8%), with additional experience across other frameworks. This distribution indicates broad cross-framework familiarity, providing a solid basis for evaluating our taxonomy’s completeness, accuracy, and usability. 

2.7 Analysis of Developer Study Results 

We analysed the developer study responses using complementary quantitative and qualitative methods to evaluate the taxonomy systematically. 

Quantitative analysis. Participants rated the practical relevance of each taxonomy element on a five-point Likert scale (1 = not relevant, 5 = highly relevant) at all levels of the hierarchy, including dimensions, categories, subcategories, and individual fault types. For each element, we computed descriptive statistics (mean, median, and standard deviation) and agreement proportions (ratings 

≥ 4). We aggregated ratings at subcategory, category, and dimension levels to examine consistency across the hierarchical structure. To assess deviation from neutrality, we compared mean ratings

against the midpoint of the scale (3.0) using one-sample tests on aggregated ratings. We also examined rating distributions across experience strata (e.g., years of agentic AI development) to assess stability across respondent groups. We evaluated internal consistency of ratings across taxonomy elements using Cronbach’s 𝛼 .

Qualitative analysis. We analysed the open-ended responses to identify limitations in taxonomy coverage and specification. Two authors independently reviewed all responses and applied inductive thematic coding to segments describing missing, unclear, or misclassified faults. They compared initial codes, resolved discrepancies through discussion, and iteratively refined a shared coding scheme. We then grouped the agreed codes into higher-level themes representing recurring coverage gaps, definition ambiguities, and structural placement issues within the taxonomy hierarchy. The resulting themes and representative examples are reported in RQ3. The authors resolved remaining disagreements through consensus. 

3 Study Results 

This section presents the findings of our study by answering three research questions. 

3.1 RQ1: What are the characteristics, observable symptoms, and root causes of bugs and failures in real-world agentic AI systems? 

Our analysis of 385 real-world faults demonstrates that failures in agentic AI systems differ funda-mentally from those in traditional software systems. Their differences are driven by the architectural demands of autonomy: agents must (i) configure and integrate an LLM-based cognitive core, (ii) maintain long-lived state across iterative control loops, and (iii) invoke external tools and services in dynamic environments. To characterise such a fault landscape, we developed a taxonomy com-prising 37 fault categories grouped into 13 major categories. We also organised them into five high-level dimensions that correspond to core agent capabilities required for autonomy [41, 43]. 

3.1.1 Taxonomy of Fault Types. I. Agent Cognition & Orchestration (83 faults). This dimension captures faults intrinsic to an agent’s cognitive architecture and control flows. Unlike conventional software failures, these failures often arise from the interaction between the model behaviour and the orchestration logic of an agentic system. 

1. LLM Integration Faults (45 faults). This category represents issues or defects at the interface be-tween an agent framework and an LLM provider, including misconfiguration, API incompatibilities, and token management issues. 

LLM Misconfiguration: This fault arises when the static configuration of an LLM provider or model is incorrect or inconsistent. It includes incorrect model identifiers, invalid parameter values (e.g., temperature or context length), incorrect file paths, or mismatched provider set-tings. Such misconfigurations prevent an agent from accessing or invoking the LLM. This fault can lead to failures such as runtime exceptions during prompt or template loading, invalid or malformed model responses, or unsuccessful LLM initialisation. For example, letta-ai/letta 

(e.g., Issue #180) specified incorrect file paths in its LLM configuration, causing a FileNot-FoundError during persona template retrieval. Similarly, FoundationAgents/MetaGPT (e.g., Issue #1336) produced invalid JSON responses when its RAG module interacted incorrectly with the Ollama model provider.

API Misuse: This fault arises when the agent violates the semantic or behavioural contract of an API or tool interface. 3.2 RQ2: Statistically Significant Associations Between Faults, Symptoms, and Root Causes in Agentic AI Systems, and Their Implications for Fault Propagation 

To understand how faults emerge and propagate in agentic AI systems, we applied association rule mining across four directed relationships: Symptom → Root Cause , Symptom → Fault Type ,

Fault Type → Symptom , and Fault Type → Root Cause . Rules were extracted using the MLxtend library [ 35 ], retaining those with confidence ≥ 0.20 and lift ≥ 5.0 following established interesting-ness criteria [15]. Agentic AI systems are stochastic, modular, and heterogeneous. As a result, dominant failure patterns are rare. Many meaningful propagation pathways occur infrequently but consistently. A moderate confidence threshold captures these stable yet non-dominant associations, while a high lift threshold filters out spurious co-occurrence. A lift of 5.0 indicates that the antecedent and consequent co-occur at least five times more often than expected under statistical independence. The resulting rule set provides a compact, statistically grounded view of fault propagation that differs from patterns typically observed in traditional software systems. Several associations are near-deterministic. Incorrect timestamps and timezone inconsistencies almost always originate from naive datetime conversions in Python (confidence = 1.00, lift = 121.0), where mixing timezone-aware and naive objects contaminates scheduling, logging, and time-based reasoning. Memory and state issues exhibit similarly strong structure. Memory-related symptoms map almost perfectly to improper state handling (confidence = 1.00, lift = 30.25), and incorrect state or memory updates consistently originate in the memory subsystem (confidence = 1.00, lift = 45.375). Because agents accumulate and reuse state across turns, corrupted state propagates forward, leading to contradictions, dropped tasks, or loss of context. Memory-related faults frequently manifest as observable memory symptoms (confidence = 0.375, lift = 27.225), making them comparatively easier to localise once surfaced. Dependency and environment faults form another prominent cluster, particularly during instal-lation, initialisation, and early execution. Dependency conflicts strongly predict transitive version incompatibilities (confidence = 0.600, lift = 31.114). Installation failures are often associated with deprecated tooling (confidence = 0.133, lift = 24.2) or version mismatches (confidence = 0.133, lift =

16.133). Import errors commonly arise from incorrect imports (confidence = 0.231, lift = 16.754) or missing dependencies (confidence = 0.154, lift = 11.169). These breakdowns obscure tool usage, reasoning steps, and state transitions, increasing time to diagnosis and amplifying downstream propagation. Cross-layer propagation recurs throughout the results. Platform incompatibilities generate execution failures (confidence = 0.333, lift = 10.083), installation failures (confidence = 0.133, lift = 8.067), and UI anomalies (confidence = 0.182, lift = 11.0). 3.3.2 Qualitative Validation and Taxonomy Refinements. In addition to ratings, respondents pro-vided qualitative feedback on missing or under-specified fault patterns. Most respondents (88/105; 83.8%) reported that the taxonomy covered their experience, while 17 respondents (16.2%) described bug types that were not fully captured. The reported gaps cluster into five refinement themes. 

(1) Non-determinism and semantic failures. Respondents distinguished between syntactic failures (outputs violate schemas and fail fast) and semantic failures (outputs are structurally valid but incorrect, inconsistent, or misaligned with ground truth). Several respondents noted that structured outputs (e.g., JSON) reduce syntax-level errors but do not prevent semantic failures (e.g., an agent asserting successful compilation when automated checks fail). These failures propagate silently through reasoning chains and often require external verifiers (tests, compilation, constraints) or human approval gates. 

(2) Multi-agent coordination failures. Respondents reported failure modes specific to multi-agent settings, including misinterpretation of inter-agent messages, coordination collapse (e.g., group-think), cascading prompt injection across agents, and higher-level deadlocks driven by circular task dependencies. While the taxonomy includes synchronisation issues, this feedback indicates a need to distinguish thread-level concurrency from agent-level coordination failures involving protocols, shared resources, and distributed task delegation. 

(3) Human-in-the-loop and workflow verification. Multiple respondents highlighted failures arising in systems that incorporate approval gates or verification checkpoints. Examples include incorrect escalation logic, hanging on human input, notification failures, and bypass of approval requirements. Respondents also emphasised automated workflow verification (tests, invariant checks, policy constraints) as a first-class mechanism, where failures occur when agents skip checks, misinterpret results, or fail to backtrack after verification failures. 

(4) Agent-specific observability gaps. While respondents endorsed logging as highly important, they emphasised that agent debugging requires more than conventional logs: practitioners need structured traces that capture decision steps, tool calls, and state transitions, and that remain recon-structable across multiple sub-agents. Respondents pointed to friction in adopting instrumentation tools and advocated for standardised trace schemas (e.g., thought/action/observation formats) to support replay, diagnosis, and auditing. 

(5) Resource exhaustion beyond token counting. Several respondents described behavioural re-source failures, such as agents failing to terminate after reaching goals, inefficient polling that inflates context windows, and runaway API usage. These issues extend beyond mechanical token counting and suggest an orchestration-level resource management refinement (budgets, backoff strategies, termination guarantees, and cost-aware control).

4 Discussion 

Our analysis of 385 faults across 40 agentic AI repositories reveals a distinct hybrid failure profile. Unlike traditional software systems, which predominantly fail due to deterministic logic errors,  

> J. ACM, Vol. 37, No. 4, Article 111. Publication date: August 2018. 111:34 Shah et al.

or standalone machine learning models, which often fail due to data quality or model limitations, agentic AI systems fail at the intersection of both paradigms. They combine conventional software components with probabilistic, language-driven reasoning, producing failure modes that neither classical software engineering nor machine learning practice alone fully addresses. In this section, we interpret our findings through a software engineering lens and discuss their broader implications. 

4.1 The Dual Nature of Agentic Faults 

Agentic AI systems cannot be characterised as purely “software” or purely “AI”; they constitute a hybrid class of systems. While cognitive failures such as hallucinations and reasoning loops are prominent in public discourse, our empirical results show that conventional software faults account for a substantial proportion of observed failures. In particular, runtime and environment grounding issues alone account for 87 cases, and logic-related faults appear in 60 instances. This duality creates fundamental debugging challenges. Traditional debuggers assume deter-ministic execution and struggle with non-deterministic LLM outputs, while AI evaluation tools typically focus on output quality and overlook structural failures such as dependency conflicts, type mismatches, or state corruption. The prevalence of logic and data-handling faults highlights a deeper mismatch between probabilistic LLM outputs and rigid programming abstractions (e.g., Python’s type system). These findings suggest that existing programming languages and tooling are poorly suited to agentic communication patterns and may need to evolve toward more flexible representations and validation mechanisms. 

4.2 The Integration Gap and Ecosystem Fragility 

Dependency and integration failures constitute the most common root cause in our dataset (19.5%), underscoring the fragility of the surrounding ecosystem. Agentic AI systems depend on a deep and rapidly evolving stack of components, including LLM provider APIs, vector databases, orchestration frameworks, and auxiliary libraries. Our results show that even minor changes—such as updates to a utility library or client SDK—can cascade into severe agent failures. We characterise this phenomenon as an integration gap : agent logic is tightly coupled to volatile external components without sufficient abstraction or insulation. This coupling renders current development practices brittle and ill-suited for mission-critical deployments. Addressing this gap will likely require stable, standardised abstraction layers that decouple agent reasoning from external services, analogous to how operating systems virtualise and manage hardware resources. 

4.3 The Observability Crisis in Autonomous Systems 

Our findings also reveal a pronounced observability deficit in agentic AI systems. System reliability and error-handling faults appear 67 times, and failures frequently manifest as silent errors or opaque messages. This lack of visibility is particularly concerning because agentic systems do not merely generate text; they invoke tools, modify external state, and act autonomously in real-world environments. Without clear execution traces, state snapshots, and structured error reporting, failures can propagate undetected across reasoning steps and tool interactions. These results indicate that observability should not be treated as an auxiliary concern but as a first-class architectural re-quirement. “Observability by design”—including structured logging, traceable reasoning steps, and auditable tool invocations—appears essential for safe and maintainable agentic systems. 

4.4 From Ad Hoc Debugging to Structural Heuristics 

Despite the apparent complexity of agentic failures, our analysis shows that fault propagation often follows strong and recurring patterns. Several associations are near-deterministic: token  

> J. ACM, Vol. 37, No. 4, Article 111. Publication date: August 2018. Characterizing Faults in Agentic AI: A Taxonomy of Types, Symptoms, and Root Causes 111:35

invalidation symptoms reliably indicate defects in local token-refresh logic; timestamp anomalies consistently stem from datetime conversion errors; and memory-related symptoms strongly predict faults in state-handling mechanisms. These patterns suggest that debugging agentic AI systems need not rely solely on ad hoc inspection. Instead, they enable the development of structural heuristics and automated checks that guide diagnosis. For example, static or runtime analysers could flag high-risk patterns (e.g., naive datetime usage, unvalidated state updates) before deployment, reducing time-to-diagnosis and failure impact. 

4.5 Implications for Software Engineering Research 

Taken together, our findings highlight several implications for software engineering research. Clas-sical unit testing is insufficient in settings where core components are stochastic and environments evolve rapidly. We identify multiple promising research directions, including: (i) mock-free integra-tion testing frameworks that simulate realistic tool interactions without brittle assumptions; (ii) probabilistic or schema-flexible type systems that better accommodate unstructured LLM outputs; and (iii) self-healing agents capable of detecting failures, diagnosing root causes, and executing structured recovery strategies. In summary, while agentic AI systems promise a new class of autonomous software, our study shows that the supporting engineering foundations remain fragile. Progress from faults to fixes 

will require treating agentic applications as complex, probabilistic systems that demand dedicated software engineering principles, tools, and abstractions. 

5 Threats to Validity 

Threats to internal validity arise from potential errors or biases in both manual analysis and auto-mated filtering. Manual annotation introduces subjectivity, which we mitigated through batch-wise analysis, an iterative coding process, and regular consensus discussions to resolve disagreements. Another threat concerns the accuracy of automated filtering performed using GPT-4.1. To address this, we evaluated the model against a human-labelled ground truth, achieving 83% accuracy and 97% recall, and manually inspected a representative subset of predictions. Finally, association rule mining may surface spurious correlations; we mitigated this risk by retaining only high-lift rules (lift ≥ 5.0), prioritising strong structural dependencies over coincidental co-occurrences. 

Threats to construct validity concern whether the taxonomy and dataset accurately capture real-world faults in agentic AI systems. To reduce noise and speculative reports, we retained only closed issues and merged pull requests, increasing confidence that the faults were repro-ducible and verified. Taxonomy completeness is another potential threat. We mitigated this by conducting a practitioner survey with developers across diverse frameworks and experience levels, which confirmed strong alignment with real-world fault patterns while also informing targeted refinements. 

Threats to external validity relate to the generalisability of our findings. Our analysis focuses on Python-based repositories, which may limit applicability to other ecosystems; however, Python currently dominates agentic AI development and deployment. To improve representativeness, we employed stratified sampling across four repository types (frameworks, libraries, tools, and applications). Additionally, we restricted selection to repositories with more than 1,000 stars and at least 30 issues, favouring mature, actively maintained projects over short-lived experimental artifacts.

6 Related Work 

Taxonomies of failures in agentic AI systems. The increasing deployment of agentic work-flows has motivated several efforts to categorise failures in such systems. Existing taxonomies generally adopt one of three perspectives: platform-centric, interaction-centric, or task-centric. From a platform-centric viewpoint, Ma et al. [ 25 ] classify failures arising from orchestration in-frastructure that manages agent lifecycles, demonstrating how platform-level constraints can cascade into system-wide crashes. In the context of multi-agent systems, Cemri et al. [ 8 ] take an interaction-centric approach, analysing emergent failures such as consensus loops and hallucinated communication protocols during inter-agent coordination. Task-centric taxonomies, including those proposed by Lu et al. [ 24 ] and Rahardja et al. [ 34 ], categorise failures based on task outcomes; Rahardja et al., in particular, focus on logic errors and syntax violations in self-debugging agents. While these studies provide valuable high-level perspectives, they primarily characterise failures in terms of execution context or task success, rather than grounding them in the underlying software architecture that implements agent behaviour. 

Failure attribution and diagnosis in agentic systems. Complementary to taxonomic efforts, recent work has examined how failures can be attributed and diagnosed in agentic workflows. Zhang et al. [ 44 ] propose a voting-based consensus mechanism to identify which agent caused a failure in multi-agent tasks, addressing the challenge of blame assignment in non-deterministic settings. Zhu et al. [ 46 ] explore self-reflective feedback loops in which agents analyse their own execution traces to refine prompts and reasoning strategies. Although effective for improving task-level performance, these approaches largely treat agents as black boxes, emphasising actor attribution or prompt refinement rather than diagnosing faults in internal software components. 

Positioning of this work. In contrast to prior studies, our work focuses on the architectural and empirical roots of failures in agentic AI software. First, we ground failures in concrete internal components—such as memory subsystems , planning and control logic , and tool interfaces —shifting the analytical focus from “who failed” to “what broke.” Second, whereas existing datasets are often benchmark- or task-specific, we analyse 385 real-world faults spanning frameworks, libraries, tools, and applications, providing coverage across the agentic AI software stack. Third, we apply Apriori-based association rule mining to quantify statistically significant relationships between symptoms, fault types, and root causes, exposing recurrent fault-propagation pathways. Finally, we validate the resulting taxonomy through a structured practitioner survey, ensuring alignment with the experiences and mental models of developers actively building and maintaining agentic AI systems. 

7 Conclusion and Future Work 

Agentic AI systems exhibit distinctive failure modes that arise from the interaction of probabilistic LLM behaviour, autonomous control loops, tool-mediated actuation, and rapidly evolving software ecosystems. This paper presents a large-scale, architecture-grounded empirical study of bugs in agentic AI applications. We analysed 13,602 closed issues and merged pull requests across 40 widely used repositories and conducted in-depth manual analysis of 385 representative faults. Using a grounded-theory approach, we derived taxonomies of fault types, symptoms, and root causes, and mapped them to core architectural components, including cognitive control and orchestration; agency and actuation; perception, context, and memory; runtime and environment grounding; and system reliability and observability. Our association rule analysis revealed high-lift fault-propagation pathways, including token invalidation symptoms pointing to token-refresh defects, timestamp anomalies indicating misuse of datetime abstractions, memory-related symptoms predicting state-handling failures, and installation

or import failures stemming from dependency and platform incompatibilities. Together, these results show that bugs in agentic AI systems differ structurally from those in traditional software: failures frequently cross boundaries between model behaviour and program logic, propagate through stateful control loops, and are amplified by fragile dependency stacks. These findings have several implications. The prevalence of many-to-many symptom–cause relationships highlights the limitations of component-local debugging and underscores the need for architecture-aware triage and observability by design. Robust agentic systems require explicit con-tracts at the model–code boundary, systematic state management across long-horizon executions, and insulation from ecosystem volatility. Future work will extend this study along several dimensions. We plan to broaden the dataset and taxonomy across programming languages, execution environments, and deployment contexts. We will investigate provider-agnostic adapters with compatibility checking, stronger model–code contracts with validation and graceful degradation, and state-safety mechanisms that support recovery in long-running control loops. We also aim to develop self-diagnosing and self-healing agents, improve observability through standardised telemetry and execution traces, and construct realistic testbeds and reliability benchmarks for agentic systems. Finally, we plan to release public benchmarks and explore automated repair techniques that map observed symptoms to likely root causes and corrective actions. By systematically characterising fault types, symptoms, and propagation pathways in real-world agentic AI software, this work contributes empirical foundations and actionable guidance for improving the reliability, diagnosability, and trustworthiness of next-generation autonomous systems.
