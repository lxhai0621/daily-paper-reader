## Abstract 

Knowledge graphs provide structured and reliable information for many real-world applications, motivating increasing interest in combining large language models (LLMs) with graph-based re-trieval to improve factual grounding. Recent Graph-based Retrieval-Augmented Generation (GraphRAG) methods therefore introduce iterative interaction between LLMs and knowledge graphs to en-hance reasoning capability. However, existing approaches typically depend on manually designed guidance and interact with knowl-edge graphs through a limited set of predefined tools, which sub-stantially constrains graph exploration. To address these limitations, we propose GraphScout, a training-centric agentic graph reasoning framework equipped with more flexible graph exploration tools. GraphScout enables models to autonomously interact with knowl-edge graphs to synthesize structured training data which are then used to post-train LLMs, thereby internalizing agentic graph reason-ing ability without laborious manual annotation or task curation. Extensive experiments across five knowledge-graph domains show that a small model (e.g., Qwen3-4B) augmented with GraphScout outperforms baseline methods built on leading LLMs (e.g., Qwen-Max) by an average of 16.7% while requiring significantly fewer in-ference tokens. Moreover, GraphScout exhibits robust cross-domain transfer performance. Our code will be made publicly available 1.

## 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) [ 1, 15 , 44 , 50 ] have achieved re-markable success in question answering and complex reasoning, but they remain prone to hallucinations and lack reliable access to domain-specific or up-to-date knowledge. Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) [ 39 ] addresses these limitations by grounding model outputs in external knowledge at inference time. While effec-tive for unstructured text, conventional RAG pipelines struggle with structured and relational data, such as knowledge graphs, where multi-hop dependencies and structural constraints are essential. This has motivated a growing interest in graph-based retrieval and reasoning (GraphRAG) [ 11 ], which seeks to exploit graph structure for relational inference and information aggregation. The first class of GraphRAG methods consists of passive retrieval-driven approaches. These methods [ 22 , 51 ] are typically built upon the standard RAG framework. They first employ static retrieval strategies to select a set of nodes from the knowledge graph that are relevant to the given query, and then expand their neighboring subgraphs according to predefined rules, such as a fixed hop count. The resulting structured information is subsequently linearized into textual form and provided to LLMs for downstream reasoning. The second class comprises active traversal-based approaches. These methods [ 23 , 40 ] equip LLMs with a set of basic graph interac-tion tools, such as node querying and relation expansion, and lever-age carefully designed prompting schemes to enable multi-round in-teractions with the knowledge graph during the reasoning process. Active traversal-based approaches enable dynamic graph explo-ration, enhancing retrieval–reasoning coordination. Despite recent progress in GraphRAG, existing methods still suf-fer from notable limitations in flexibility and graph exploration. On the one hand, most current approaches rely on manually designed and limited sets of graph interaction primitives, which constrains their expressiveness and flexibility. For example, when performing multi-hop neighbor queries along known path types, models often have to invoke basic neighbor expansion operations iteratively, resulting in prolonged interaction sequences and low efficiency. On the other hand, LLMs lack intrinsic priors for structured graph exploration, causing these methods to primarily depend on exter-nal mechanisms, such as workflow constraints, to enhance graph exploration. As a result, they struggle to achieve a fundamental improvement in the LLM’s intrinsic graph reasoning and explo-ration capabilities. Empirical results reported in prior work (e.g., GraphCoT [ 23 ] in Figure 1) also reflect these limitations to some extent. Even with more flexible graph exploration tools, these issues remain unresolved. For example, PolyG [ 33 ] adopts a Cypher-based interface to enable more general graph operations, yet its perfor-mance (in Figure 1) only marginally surpasses that of GraphCoT due to its limited intrinsic graph reasoning and exploration abilities. These observations suggest that current GraphRAG research calls for a shift in methodological perspective. Rather than continu-ally stacking increasingly complex prompt engineering or workflow control mechanisms, a more promising direction is to equip LLMs with more flexible and expressive graph interaction tools, while fundamentally enhancing their intrinsic graph reasoning and explo-ration abilities. Nevertheless, enhancing such intrinsic capabilities typically requires targeted post-training, which in turn relies on diverse, high-quality annotated graph reasoning trajectories that are expensive to manually construct and difficult to scale in practice, forming a major bottleneck. To address the above challenges, we propose GraphScout , a training-centric framework designed to equip LLM with intrinsic exploration ability for agentic graph reasoning. GraphScout con-sists of three core components. First, GraphScout provides a set of Agentic Graph Exploration Tools , including a Code Inter-preter and a Node Retriever, which enable LLMs to interact with the knowledge graph in a highly flexible and programmable manner. Second, the Graph Quizzer module leverages a strong LLM as a senior scout to freely explore the graph environment, automatically synthesizing diverse and high-quality graph query–answer pairs, along with their corresponding evidence clues that serve as super-vision signals for subsequent training. Finally, the Graph Solver 

module optimizes small-parameter LLM as a junior scout under a multi-turn interaction paradigm, enabling it to progressively ac-quire agentic graph reasoning ability. Experiments show that LLMs trained with GraphScout consistently outperform baselines built on substantially larger backbone LLMs on multiple GraphRAG bench-marks by an average margin of 16.7% , while requiring an order of magnitude fewer inference tokens. Moreover, models trained on a single-domain knowledge graph transfer robustly to unseen domains, demonstrating stable cross-domain performance. ## 2 Related Work 2.1 LLM for Graph Reasoning 

Inspired by the recent success of large language models (LLMs) in natural language processing, increasing attention has been paid to applying LLMs to graph-related tasks [ 22 ]. Existing studies can be broadly categorized according to the role LLMs play in graph reasoning. One line of work treats LLMs as feature extractors [ 6,7, 10 , 29 , 52 ], where textual information associated with graph nodes or edges is encoded by LLMs and then fed into graph neural networks (GNNs) [ 27 , 45 , 49 ]. These methods typically adopt an LLM-GNN cascaded architecture, leveraging LLMs for semantic representation and GNNs for structural modeling. Another line of research employs LLMs as final predictors [ 24 ]. In this paradigm, graph structures are either serialized into textual sequences and processed directly by LLMs [ 4, 47 ], or combined with GNNs that are specifically trained or fine-tuned to provide structural supervision for LLM-based reasoning [ 2, 5 , 41 ]. ## 2.2 Augmenting LLMs with Knowledge Graph 

Despite their strong reasoning capabilities [1, 44, 50], LLMs suffer from limitations such as hallucinations [ 36 , 43 ] and outdated knowl-edge, motivating retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) [ 3, 14 , 25 ]. While effective for unstructured text, conventional RAG strug-gles with relational knowledge [ 28 , 37 ]. To address this gap, re-cent work augments LLMs with knowledge graphs, giving rise to GraphRAG [ 11 ]. Existing approaches can be broadly categorized into two categories. The first follows a passive retrieval-driven paradigm, retrieving relevant nodes or subgraphs and linearizing them into textual representations for LLM consumption [ 4, 11 , 18 ]. The second line of work adopts an active traversal-based paradigm, enabling LLMs to iteratively interact with knowledge graphs by selecting traversal operations step by step [ 23 , 40 ]. Representative methods include GraphCoT [ 23 ], which aligns chain-of-thought reasoning with stepwise graph traversal. More recent approaches further extend this paradigm through structured planning or agent collaboration [ 13 , 26 , 32 , 33 ], improving robustness and accuracy in complex graph reasoning. PolyG [ 33 ] generates Cypher queries conditioned on the question structure, while GraphCounselor [ 13 ]leverages multi-agent coordination for planning and verification. Despite these advances, such methods still rely on manually de-signed interaction schemes and external control mechanisms, rather than learning intrinsic graph reasoning and exploration capabilities. 

## 3 GraphScout 

As illustrated in Figure 2, GraphScout overcomes the limitations of predefined tools by integrating a code interpreter and a node re-triever as Agentic Graph Exploration Tools for both Graph Quizzer and Graph Solver. Graph Quizzer is designed to explore high-quality graph question-answer pairs for LLM post-training, employing a strong LLM as a senior scout to construct diverse graph problems along with their corresponding traversal clues. Graph Solver is pro-posed to endow LLMs with intrinsic exploration by post-training a small-parameter LLM as a junior scout, formulating graph rea-soning as learning a multi-turn decision policy for agentic graph interaction. Training is primarily driven by answer rewards, with auxiliary clue-based rewards to guide exploration under sparse feedback. Consequently, the overall GraphScout framework com-prehensively explores diverse graph reasoning problems through the Graph Quizzer and efficiently addresses intrinsic graph explo-ration challenges via the Graph Solver. ## 3.1 Preliminary 

Formally, a knowledge graph is denoted by K ⊆ E × R × E , where 

E is the set of entities and R is the set of relations. A fact in a knowledge graph is commonly represented as a triple (ℎ, 𝑟, 𝑡 ) for convenience, where ℎ, 𝑡 ∈ E . The graph-based question answering task under a retrieval-augmented setting is defined as 

𝑓 : Q × K → Y , (1) where Q is the set of graph reasoning questions in natural lan-guage [ 23 ] and Y is the set of answers. The graph reasoning process of an LLM can be modeled as a policy 𝜋 (𝑎 𝑡 | 𝑞, 𝑐 𝑡 ), which selects the next action 𝑎 𝑡 given the input question 𝑞 and the historical reasoning trajectory 𝑐 𝑡 . Here, 𝜋 (·) is the policy distribution of an LLM, 𝑞 ∈ Q is a question, 𝑎 𝑡 ∈ A is an action selection at step 𝑡 ,

𝑐 𝑡 = (𝑜 1, 𝑎 1, · · · , 𝑜 𝑡 −1, 𝑎 𝑡 −1, 𝑜 𝑡 ) is the contextual observation-action sequence. When the process reaches an answer, the reasoning tra-jectory can be denoted as 𝜏 = (𝑜 1, 𝑎 1, · · · , 𝑎 𝑇 , 𝑜 𝑇 ), where 𝑇 is the number of reasoning steps and 𝑜 1 = 𝑞 . Existing prompting-based methods [ 13 , 26 , 32 , 33 ] rely on external guidance and primarily focus on enriching the action space A with more hand-crafted tools and auxiliary prompts. However, a predefined set of tools A and a fixed policy 𝜋 (·) significantly limit the exploration potential of LLMs. Therefore, our goal is to unlock the intrinsic exploration ca-pability of LLMs by incorporating flexible exploration tools Agraph 

and learning an optimized policy 𝜋 𝜃 (·) for graph reasoning. 

## 3.2 Agentic Graph Exploration Tools 

As the adage states, “If a craftsman wants to do his work well, he must first sharpen his tools”. Existing GraphRAG methods typically rely on a predefined set of tools, such as retrieving neighboring nodes, checking relations, or extracting node attributes. While sim-ple, these approaches struggle with neighborhood explosion and multi-hop collaborative patterns. In contrast, GraphScout exposes an agentic graph exploration interface Agraph , which integrates two complementary tools: a Code Interpreter for high-order pattern analysis and a Node Retriever for global graph retrieval. This agentic interface is invoked proactively by the LLM, formatted as 

<tool_call> { "name":"tool_name", "arguments":{"tool_arguments"}} </tool_call>. 

3.2.1 Code Interpreter . The Code Interpreter provides a safe Python execution environment with access to a Cypher-based graph query interface, allowing the model to write executable code for precise and compositional graph queries. Through this interface, the model can flexibly control query logic, process intermediate results, and adapt its exploration strategy based on the evolving interaction context, rather than following predefined traversal templates. In our implementation, large-scale knowledge graphs are stored in a Neo4j database. During a preprocessing stage, each dataset is imported into Neo4j and equipped with appropriate schema-level and property-level indexes, enabling efficient execution of Cypher queries. 

3.2.2 Node Retriever . The Node Retriever is implemented as a FAISS-based vector search module that supports fuzzy entity grounding. Given textual mentions in questions or intermediate hy-potheses, it retrieves candidate node identifiers based on semantic similarity, enabling robust mapping from language to graph entities prior to structured querying. By combining a programmable Code Interpreter with a light-weight Node Retriever, GraphScout extends the action space as 

A = Agraph ∪ L , where L is the intrinsic verbal reasoning actions of an LLM. Therefore, GraphScout significantly expands the explo-ration space available to the LLM while keeping the tool interface minimal. More details are provided in Appendix B.4.1. 

## 3.3 Graph Quizzer 

Using these agentic graph exploration tools Agraph , Graph Quizzer employs a strong LLM as a senior scout to flexibly explore graph problems Qtrain at varying difficulty levels according to general task specifications. As shown in the upper panel of Figure 2, Graph Quizzer explores the higher-order neighborhoods and global graph semantics by alternating verbal reasoning and tool use until a stop-ping condition is met. Through this exploration loop, Graph Quizzer generates high-quality graph question-answering pairs Dtrain =

(Q train , Ytrain , Ctrain ) for subsequent LLM post-training, effectively eliminating laborious dataset curation. We explicitly record inter-mediate node clues Ctrain = {𝑐 |𝑐 = (𝑒 𝑖 , 𝑒 𝑗 , · · · , 𝑒 𝑘 ) and 𝑒 𝑖 ∈ E} to mitigate reward sparsity during the ensuing training process. 

3.3.1 Task specification . Each quizzer episode begins by sam-pling an exploration objective that specifies the structural and se-mantic requirements of the graph question to be generated. Rather than relying on using fixed templates, objectives are specified at an abstract level to guide task-oriented exploration while preserving flexibility. Each objective is characterized by a combination of an-swer type , query pattern , and difficulty , which together ensure diversity among generated questions. 

Answer type. The answer type defines the expected form of the final answer and the corresponding supervision signal. We consider four common types: entity (identifying a graph entity), boolean 

(judging whether a given statement is true or false), number (count-ing or aggregation), and set (a collection of entities). This diversity encourages the quizzer to produce supervision beyond simple entity retrieval. 

Query pattern. The query pattern specifies the abstract struc-tural form of a graph query, indicating which components are given and which must be discovered through exploration. Following PolyG [ 33 ], we adopt standard patterns including ⟨ℎ, _, _⟩, ⟨ℎ, 𝑟, ∗⟩ ,

⟨ℎ, ∗, 𝑡 ⟩, and ⟨ℎ, 𝑟, 𝑡 ⟩, as well as hybrid patterns that combine multi-ple forms to support more compositional reasoning. 

Difficulty. The difficulty level controls the required reasoning depth and is categorized as simple , medium , or hard , corresponding to single-hop to deeper multi-hop reasoning. Varying difficulty allows quizzer to generate exploration trajectories with different lengths and structural complexity. Together, these three factors define a structured yet flexible objective space that enables Graph Quizzer to cover diverse graph reasoning behaviors without relying on rigid, hand-crafted task templates. 3.3.2 Exploration initialization . Given a sampled exploration objective, Graph Quizzer initializes the scout with a compact en-vironment context 𝑐 env and a concrete seed node 𝑒 𝑖 , providing the minimal context required for task-oriented exploration. 

Graph description. The scout is provided with a concise de-scription of the target knowledge graph, including core schema-level information such as node types, edge types, and commonly used properties. This description also specifies the underlying explo-ration tools Agraph , grounding the scout’s subsequent interactions in a concrete execution environment. 

Seed node. Each exploration episode is anchored at a seed node 

𝑒 𝑖 ∈ E sampled from the target graph. To mitigate bias from im-balanced node-type distributions, we adopt a two-stage sampling strategy: first uniformly sampling a node type, followed by random selection of a node instance within that type. This strategy ensuring balanced coverage across graph structures and reasoning scenarios. 

3.3.3 Exploration process . Starting from the initialized seed node 𝑒 𝑖 , the scout performs a bounded, multi-step exploration pro-cess conditioned on the sampled exploration objective. Exploration proceeds as an iterative explore-assess loop, in which the scout actively retrieves graph evidence and continuously evaluates its progress toward satisfying the objective. At each step, the scout formulates a concrete retrieval action, such as inspecting neighboring relations, expanding along selected paths, or aggregating graph statistics, and executes it through the graph interaction interface. The returned results are summarized into a local exploration state, which is used to assess whether the accumulated evidence is sufficient to meet the specified answer type, query pattern, and difficulty constraints. This process is explicitly task-oriented: rather than exhaustively traversing the graph K, the senior scout prioritizes a partial rea-soning trajectory 𝑐 𝑡 that is most relevant to the current objective and terminates exploration early once sufficient supporting infor-mation has been collected. To prevent unbounded interaction, the exploration length is capped by a fixed budget of tool calls (we use 

𝑇 =10 ), encouraging efficient and focused exploration behaviors. 

3.3.4 Question reporting . Once the senior scout determines that the exploration objective has been satisfied, it transitions from the question-exploration stage to the question-reporting stage. Using the collected reasoning trajectory 𝑐 𝑇 , the senior scout constructs a natural-language graph question 𝑞 together with its corresponding answer 𝑦 , ensuring that 𝑦 is directly verifiable on the underlying graph. In addition to the final question-answer pair (𝑞, 𝑦 ), Graph Quizzer records a set of evidence clues 𝑐 = (𝑒 𝑖 , 𝑒 𝑗 , · · · , 𝑒 𝑘 ), each ele-ment being a node identifier explicitly used during question and answer formation. These clues provide an explicit link between the generated answer 𝑦 and concrete graph entities 𝑒 𝑖 encountered during exploration. As a result, each quizzer episode generates a structured supervision tuple: ( question , answer , clue_nodes ), where the question 𝑞 is expressed in natural language, the answer 𝑦 

is grounded in the graph, and the evidence clues 𝑐 anchor supervi-sion to explicit graph-level facts. This design enables downstream models to learn not only to produce correct answers, but also to align their reasoning processes with relevant evidence clues. 

## 3.4 Graph Solver 

Graph Solver further trains a small-parameter LLM as a junior scout 

to tackle the graph-reasoning questions Qtrain proposed by Graph Quizzer, using reward signals for answer correctness and alignment with evidence clues. As shown in the bottom panel of Figure 2, the junior scout iteratively reasons the possible solution paths via explo-ration tools Agraph in a multi-turn paradigm until it outputs a final answer. The reasoning policy can be denoted by 𝜋 𝜃 (𝜏 |𝑞 ), where 

𝜋 𝜃 (·) is the parameterized LLM and 𝜏 is the trajectory involving verbal reasoning steps and tool actions. This simple yet effective design empowers LLMs to perform intrinsic exploration over ques-tions Qtrain and complex graphs K, going beyond the limited tools and static prompts used in existing GraphRAG methods. 

3.4.1 Problem formulation . Graph Solver formulates graph ques-tion answering as learning a multi-turn decision policy for task-oriented interaction with a knowledge graph. Given a natural lan-guage question 𝑞 , the model must decide how to sequentially invoke tools Agraph to retrieve evidence, aggregate information, and ulti-mately produce an answer. Formally, each episode corresponds to a trajectory 𝜏 = (𝑜 1, 𝑎 1, 𝑜 2, 𝑎 2, . . . , 𝑜 𝑇 , 𝑎 𝑇 ) of alternating observations and actions, where the initial observation is the question itself, i.e., 

𝑜 1 = 𝑞 . Actions {𝑎 𝑡 }𝑇 𝑡 =1 are expressed as tool calls, and observa-tions {𝑜 𝑡 }𝑇 𝑡 =1 include structured tool responses and the model’s intermediate verbal reasoning. The objective is not to follow prede-fined traversal rules, but to acquire a generalizable solver strategy 

𝜋 𝜃 (𝜏 | 𝑞 ) that coordinates entity grounding, graph traversal, and answer synthesis under a fixed interaction budget.

## 5 Conclusion 

This paper presents GraphScout, a framework that enhances LLMs’ agentic graph reasoning with intrinsic exploration ability. Graph-Scout consists of three core components: Agentic Graph Exploration Tools, which equip LLMs with more flexible and programmable graph interaction interface; Graph Quizzer, which explores diverse and high-quality problem sets on graphs while recording traversal clues; and Graph Solver, which post-trains LLMs to acquire intrin-sic graph exploration ability. Extensive experiments against state-of-the-art GraphRAG methods demonstrate that small-parameter LLMs equipped with GraphScout significantly outperforms substan-tially larger LLMs by an average of 16.7%. Moreover, GraphScout exhibits promising cross-domain generalization capability, indicat-ing that LLMs can develop generalized graph reasoning ability even with training on a single graph. Future work will investigate LLMs bootstrapping via iterative self-questioning and self-answering in an iterative enhancement loop, while addressing challenges such as training instability and self-bias amplification.

In total, GRBENCH contains 1,740 manually designed question-answer pairs, all in English, covering three difficulty levels: easy, medium, and hard. Each question can be answered by explicitly reasoning over the corresponding domain graph rather than re-lying solely on parametric knowledge. also-bought , buy-after-viewing , and bought-together . This domain emphasizes large-scale, densely connected graphs and supports aggregation-based reasoning tasks. ## A.4 Details of Baseline 

In this section, we provide detailed descriptions of all baseline methods evaluated in our experiments, including their underly-ing assumptions, graph interaction mechanisms, and reasoning paradigms. All baselines are implemented following their origi-nal papers and, unless otherwise specified, are evaluated under the same evaluation protocols as GraphScout to ensure fair comparison. 

BaseLLM. BaseLLM serves as a non-graph baseline to assess the extent to which LLMs can answer domain-specific questions relying solely on their internal parametric knowledge. For this baseline, the model is prompted directly with the question, without access to any external graph or retrieved context. 

TextRAG. TextRAG [ 14 ] treats the knowledge graph as a textual corpus by linearizing node attributes and edge information into plain text. A dense retriever is employed to fetch the most relevant text chunks based on semantic similarity with the input question, and the retrieved text is appended to the prompt for answer gener-ation. 

GraphRAG. GraphRAG [ 51 ] extends TextRAG by incorporating structural information during retrieval. Instead of retrieving iso-lated text chunks, GraphRAG retrieves subgraphs centered around core entities identified from the question, typically using 2-hop neighborhood expansion. The retrieved subgraphs are then lin-earized into text sequences and provided as context to the LLM. 

Cypher. The Cypher baseline directly queries the underlying knowledge graph using Cypher. For each question, the LLM is prompted to generate a Cypher query, which is executed against the graph to retrieve relevant nodes or attributes. The query results are then returned to the LLM for answer synthesis. To adapt this method to the tasks considered in this paper, we additionally incorporate a node retrieval module to identify candidate nodes prior to Cypher query execution. 

GraphCoT. GraphCoT [ 23 ] is a reasoning-based GraphRAG method that enables multi-step interaction between the LLM and the graph through a predefined set of graph traversal tools. The model incrementally explores the graph, retrieves intermediate ev-idence, and updates its reasoning context in a chain-of-thought manner. By decomposing complex queries into multiple reasoning steps, GraphCoT mitigates information loss caused by long lin-earized contexts and serves as a strong iterative reasoning baseline. 

PolyG. PolyG [ 33 ] introduces an adaptive graph traversal strat-egy that dynamically adjusts exploration paths based on the evolv-ing reasoning state. Unlike fixed-hop retrieval methods, PolyG allows the model to select different graph operators conditioned on the query and intermediate observations. This flexibility improves coverage for diverse question types, particularly those requiring non-uniform traversal patterns. To adapt this method to the tasks considered in this paper, we additionally incorporate a node re-trieval module to identify candidate nodes prior to graph traversal. 

GraphCounselor. GraphCounselor [ 13 ] employs a multi-agent collaborative framework for graph reasoning. It decomposes the reasoning process into planning, execution, and reflection stages, allowing the model to dynamically adjust reasoning depth and correct earlier mistakes through self-reflection. 

LLM Backbones. To ensure robustness and generalizability, all baselines are instantiated with multiple state-of-the-art founda-tion models, including GPT-4o [1], GLM-4.6 [15], Qwen-Max [50], and DeepSeek-Chat [ 31 ]. Unless otherwise noted, decoding param-eters (e.g., temperature and top-𝑝 ) follow the default or recom-mended settings from the corresponding baseline implementations. In addition, for all methods that require vector retrieval, we use 

Qwen3-text-embedding-v4 to obtain text embeddings. Overall, these baselines cover a broad spectrum of graph rea-soning paradigms, ranging from text-only retrieval and symbolic querying to iterative and adaptive graph exploration.

To analyze the diversity of the data generated by Graph Quizzer, we perform an additional automatic annotation analysis on the gener-ated questions. Specifically, during this analysis, we use DeepSeek-Chat as the judging LLM, while deliberately withholding the per-question task configurations assigned during generation, including the specific combinations of difficulty levels, question structural patterns, and answer types specified for each individual question. Instead, we only provide the definitions of these categories together with each generated question, allowing the model to independently determine the task category combination that each question actu-ally corresponds to. This de-conditioned annotation strategy is designed to analyze the generated data from the perspective of the questions’ intrinsic semantics and structures. On the one hand, during generation, Graph Quizzer may not always strictly adhere to the predefined task instructions; on the other hand, this post hoc annotation process enables us to explicitly verify the actual task attributes reflected by each question at the semantic level. B.2.3 Why GraphCoT fails but GraphScout succeeds . The failure of GraphCoT mainly stems from a tool-to-schema mismatch 

and the lack of a robust recovery strategy. Specifically, Graph-CoT assumes that “Aphasia, Primary Progressive” is a Disease 

node and repeatedly queries non-existent neighbor types (e.g., 

Disease-downregulates-Gene , Disease-associates-Gene ). Once the tool reports that the neighbor type does not exist, GraphCoT keeps retrying with the same (incorrect) schema assumption, and eventually concludes that the graph contains no relevant informa-tion, leading to an incorrect termination. In contrast, GraphScout exhibits a two-level advantage that com-bines reasoning and tool usage . On the reasoning level, it ex-plicitly diagnoses the entity-type mismatch (the retrieved node is a Symptom , not a Disease ) and formulates a corrective plan: first resolve the underlying disease(s) that present the symptom, then query downregulated genes, and finally aggregate cellular compo-nents. ## B.3 Prompt Design for Graph Quizzer 

Graph Quizzer relies on a carefully structured prompting scheme to guide a strong language model to perform task-oriented yet flexible exploration over an open and programmable graph interface. In-stead of using rigid question templates, the prompts specify abstract task objectives , including question pattern, answer type, and diffi-culty level, while leaving the concrete exploration strategy to the LLM. This design encourages diverse graph exploration behaviors while ensuring that the collected evidence can be reliably grounded in the underlying knowledge graph. Base System Prompt for Graph Quizzer You are a graph-analysis agent operating on a heteroge-neous knowledge graph stored in Neo4j. Your objective is to perform multi-round, task-oriented ex-ploration to collect graph evidence that supports a specific question pattern, answer type, and difficulty constraint. You interact with the graph through a programmable Python sandbox with access to a read-only Cypher in-terface. Your exploration should be: 

• Task-oriented : focus on evidence relevant to the current task specification. 

• Incremental : gather information over multiple rounds rather than exhaustively traversing the graph. 

• Verifiable : ensure that all collected evidence can be reproduced via explicit Cypher queries. Once sufficient evidence has been collected, you should stop exploration and construct exactly one graph reasoning question together with its answer. 

B.3.1 Difficulty-Specific Exploration Prompts . controls the expected depth and structural complexity of exploration, ranging from shallow, local queries to multi-hop, compositional reasoning over heterogeneous graph structures. It determines the allowed traversal depth, the number of intermediate entities, and whether hybrid reasoning patterns are permitted. Difficulty Level: SIMPLE 

Design Goal. Encourage shallow, interpretable graph ex-ploration that can be resolved with minimal reasoning steps. 

Exploration Constraints. 

• Restrict traversal depth to 1–2 hops. 

• Prefer a single dominant relationship type. 

• Avoid compositional or hybrid reasoning patterns. 

Evidence Scope. Collected evidence should support direct fact retrieval or simple aggregation, and be sufficient to answer the question without multi-step inference. 

Typical Use Cases. Single-hop entity lookup, attribute retrieval, or basic neighborhood queries. Difficulty Level: MEDIUM 

Design Goal. Promote controlled multi-step reasoning while maintaining clear semantic structure. 

Exploration Constraints. 

• Explore 2–4 hop paths with explicit intermediate nodes. 

• Involve at least two distinct relationship types or node types. 

• Allow limited use of filtering, ranking, or property constraints.

Typical Use Cases. Multi-hop entity discovery, con-strained aggregation, or relational queries requiring in-termediate reasoning. Difficulty Level: HARD

Exploration Constraints. 

• Allow hybrid or combined question patterns.
